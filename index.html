// THE BOOK
Washington, D.C. May 1987.
A 22-year-old journalist finds a floppy disk in a McDonald's bag.
What's on it will destroy a defense contractor—
if it doesn't destroy him first.

Daniel Murcer arrives in Washington on a Saturday with four hundred dollars, a trunk full of boxes, and a job at the Meridian Institute for Policy Research that starts Monday. He stops at a McDonald's outside Hazleton, Pennsylvania. He orders a Quarter Pounder and apple pie. He doesn't know that the girl behind the register—a nineteen-year-old from Hazleton named Marie Kowalski—has just hidden a floppy disk in his bag.

The disk belongs to her brother Tommy, sixteen, a programmer who built something he shouldn't have: a two-stage encryption payload capable of making the Defense Logistics Agency's entire data infrastructure inaccessible. He calls it CIPHER. A defense contractor called Cerberus Systems has taken Tommy to finish the work. They are running out of time.

Daniel has four days before the program deploys.

ONE DISK is a literary techno-thriller set in May 1987—a world of pay phones, floppy disks, government procurement, and a newsroom that still uses pencils. It is a story about what it costs to publish the truth before someone decides you shouldn't get the chance.

CHAPTER ONE — SOUTHBOUND

The Accord smelled like old coffee and carpet. Daniel Murcer had known the smell so long he had stopped noticing it three hours outside Syracuse. The back seat held two cardboard boxes, a duffel bag, and the disorder that accumulates at the end of four years: a lamp without a shade, a damp shower caddy, a framed photograph that had never found a wall and was making another attempt in the dark.

He had Tracy Kidder in the passenger seat and The Joshua Tree in the deck and four hundred dollars in his wallet, which was the right amount to begin something new with, provided nothing went wrong in the next three days.

He was twenty-two years old and had a job that started Monday and had not yet learned that these two facts were related.

CHAPTER TWO — HAZLETON

The McDonald's was the same McDonald's it always was, which was the point of McDonald's.

He ordered without looking at the menu. Quarter Pounder. Medium fries. Coffee. The girl behind the register punched it in before he finished saying it. Her name tag said Marie. She had dark hair pulled back and the stillness of someone paying more attention than she appeared to be. He noticed this without meaning to: she was too sharp for the room.

"Anything else?"

He glanced up at the menu board. "Apple pie."

"Those take a few minutes. I'll bring it over."

// THE AUTHOR
AC

Aaron Christopher writes literary thrillers about the spaces between institutional power and individual conscience.

ONE DISK, his debut novel, is set in Washington, D.C. in May 1987, and draws on research into defense procurement, early computing, government oversight, and the specific weight of decisions made before anyone is watching.

He is the author of Lake Effect.

Period Accuracy

ONE DISK is set in May 1987. Every technology, institution, and cultural reference in the novel is period-accurate.

Daniel uses IBM XT and AT computers. The disk is a 3.5-inch Sony floppy—newer in 1987, consistent with a technically sophisticated teenager. CIPHER's architecture—a two-stage payload hiding a keyfile in sectors outside the FAT directory structure—is technically plausible for 1987 hardware and a programmer working alone.

The Defense Logistics Agency operated out of Cameron Station, Virginia and leased space at New Carrollton. The GSA scheduling system existed. The House Defense Oversight Subcommittee existed. Defense Systems Weekly is fictional, but the publication category was real.

Paul Simon's Graceland was released in August 1986. U2's The Joshua Tree was released in March 1987. Both appear in the novel because Daniel Murcer is driving south in May 1987 with the right music for the decade he's about to leave behind.
